The **Schwarzschild radius** is the critical distance from the center of a massive body where the gravitational pull becomes so strong that not even light can escape, defining the boundary of a black hole.

It is the critical size an object with a mass must be before collapsing into a black hole.

The Schwarzschild radius was named after the German astronomer [Karl Schwarzschild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Schwarzschild), who calculated this exact solution for the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity) in 1916.

The Schwarzschild radius is given as
$$
r_{s}=\frac{2GM}{c^2}
$$
where $G$ is the gravitational constant, $M$ is the object mass, and $c$ is the speed of light.

For a mass as small as a human being, the Schwarzschild radius is of the order of $10^{-23}$ cm, much smaller than the nucleus of an atom; for a typical star such as the Sun, it is about 3 km (2 miles).
