==CONCLUSION==
Based on the data presented above, the following conclusions have been formulated:
1. The powdered form of dried S. rebaudiana leaves—as a natural sweetener—is significantly different from commercially available table sugar in terms of sweetness, availability, calorie count, and solubility.
2. The use of powdered Stevia with coffee—in place of table sugar—has also evidently proven to lower the blood sugar levels of hyperglycemic individuals.
3. The anticariogenic properties of Stevia powder has also been proven by the use of a simple experiment.
4. The use of Stevia as an alternative cooking ingredient has also been proven in contrast with table sugar. It has also been concluded that powdered Stevia is much more sweeter than sugar.

==RECOMMENDATIONS==
The following recommendations are presented to the future researchers of this study:

1. Instead of a jelly candy, powdered Stevia could be applied to beverages or cuisine.
2. Recommendations are made to extend the trial periods of the anti-diabetic experiments with coffee (to 30 days at least); to assess whether the continuous usage of Stevia influences the normalization of the FBS and PPBS levels of the participants. The results should then be compared with the findings of Nema et al. (2020).
3. It is recommended to use Stevia alongside antihyperglycemic medications—as with Nema et al. (2020)—and assess whether it influences the blood glucose levels of the participants.
4. Instead of coffee, the experiment is also recommended to use herbal tea instead.
5. It is highly suggested to select an equal number of participants per age group in the survey—to avoid any inconsistencies—as the age groups were not considered prior to the dissemination of the survey.
6. It is also recommended to use 170 g of sugar in the candy making process. That way, the survey will attain a more fair judgement amongst the taste assessment comparison of both candies; as the lack in sugar made the candy in this study unfavourable and a little unfair for the taste parameter.
