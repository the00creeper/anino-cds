To think about growing up is to think about the partial death of oneself. It is an act of human nature; an inevitable act of change. If there was one piece of literature that had brought light to this sophisticated stage of human life in an enchanting manner, it would be the all-time classic—The Little Prince.

The Little Prince was written in a first person point of view, where the narrator himself was a character in the story—a pilot that had crashed his plane onto the hot dunes of the Sahara Desert.  This was a reference to the author which also happened to be a pilot of the French Air Force during World War II. I like how the author talks to the fourth wall all the time. It was not like the author had only thought of the existence of the little prince in his mind which he then wrote down in the form of a book; he talks as if he was telling a story about a little boy that he had actually met in real life, six years ago, and that he wrote the book as a way of immortalizing his encounters with him.
