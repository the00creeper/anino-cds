
The kabyawan is a stone device used to crush sugar canes to extract its juice for making molasses or sugar. It is operated by a carabao that moves in a circular motion relative to its center, which spins the two stones where the sugar cane is fed in between to crush them.

History
Kabyawan is derived from the Malay word of the same name (kabyawan: gilingan). The use of the kabyawan was prevalent among natives constituting mainly the ancient tribes of the Tagalogs that hails from Bulacan and Capampangan. It is speculated that the kabyawan tools might have been brought by the exploring Malays and later improved by the natives settling in the island of Luzon.

Influence
The southern Nueva Ecija town of Cabiao was the namesake of the kabyawan. The kabyawan became familiar in the place due to its growing popularity and was named “Kabyawan,” later shortened to “Kabyaw,” and eventually to its altered spelling today due to the influence of the foreign alphabet. The Kabyawan Festival in Cabiao is annually celebrated to the feast of the town patron Saint John Nepomocene from May 8 to May 16.
