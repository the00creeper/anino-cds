Table 1.1.
Table 1.1 presents the results of Experiments 1 and 2, as mentioned in the “Material and Methodology” section of this paper. The normal FBS level of an adult is around 70–100 mg/dL, while the normal PPBS level should not exceed 180 mg/dL (Kanmani, 2018). The use of Stevia powder was not able to set the FBS levels of the participants down to the normal values as a whole, and only had a mean value of 101.5 mg/dL. However, it did show a significant reduction on both the FBS and PPBS levels of the respondents as opposed to the continuous usage of sugar. There is also a notable difference on the FBS and PPBS means of both treatments, especially with the latter—supporting the fact that Stevia is a non-caloric sweetener.

Table 2.1.
Table 2.1 represents the sweetness and taste assays on the Stevia jelly candy compared with the sugar jelly candy on different age groups. It was conducted to demonstrate that Stevia is 10–15 times sweeter than sugar, as stated by Basharat et al., 2021.  Less than half of the respondents (47%) were from the age group 15–22. The same age group also had the highest “No. of participants” to “Overall Taste mean” ratio at 548%, as opposed to the age group that got the highest “Overall Taste mean” of 2.60 (10–14), with a “No. of participants” to “Overall Taste mean” ratio of only 192%.
[new paragraph]
A majority of the respondents describe Stevia as bitter on the first bite, but will gradually become sweeter and sweeter the longer it stays on the mouth. The same amount of sugar and Stevia (17 g) was used on both candies. And although the Stevia jelly candy only yielded a mean value of 2.43 in sweetness, it was still far greater than that of sugar with only 1.07. For contrast, the original devised recipe for the sugar jelly candy used 170 g of sugar, and only then can sugar match the sweetness of Stevia. For the sake of fairness, however, the amount of sugar used was agreed to be equalized to that of Stevia. The amount in which sugar can become recognizably sweet in this recipe (170 g) is 10 times greater than that of Stevia (17 g), therefore supporting the claim of Basharat et al. The insufficiency in sugar also explained why the gelatine in the candy became soggy and slimy. Hence, placing it out of the participants’ favour in terms of taste.

Table 3.
A summary of Table 6 shows that more sugar dissolved in boiling water than that of Stevia. The intial amount of water before being subjected to heat was 400 mL. It was left to boil on low heat with an open lid until it reached its boiling point after 10 min and 20 sec, which by then, has been reduced down to 310 mL due to evaporation.
[new paragraph]
The total amount of dissolved solutes was based on the amount of water after reaching its saturation point whilst boiling. Therefore, 174.05 grams (41 tsp.) of sugar was able to dissolve in 210 mL of water while boiling for 21 minutes, and only 66.45 grams (30 tsp.) of Stevia was able to dissolve in 200 mL of water while boiling for 15 minutes. This goes to show that a sugar-water solution presents a higher saturation point than a Stevia-water solution, and that sugar is more soluble than powdered Stevia in water. Therefore, the null hypothesis is rejected and the alternative hypothesis is accepted. Thus, this study reveals the significant difference between the solubility of sugar and Stevia powder in a control water solvent.

Sweetness
Stevia-based sweeteners may come in various forms. The white crystalline powder form of Stevia is said to be approximately 300 times sweeter than sucrose (Gasmalla, et al., 2014), and is used primarily on large-scale production from big corporations. Furthermore, according to Singh & Rao, 2005, the powdered form of S. rebaudiana leaves is 15–20 times sweeter than that of sucrose in table sugar. This study by Singh & Rao has been evidently proven later on in this study, by the use of a comparison (survey) between a jelly candy that used sugar and a jelly candy that used Stevia (see Table 3 and its interpretation).
