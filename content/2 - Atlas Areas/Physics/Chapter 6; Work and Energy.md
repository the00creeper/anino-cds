Work is done on an object when a force acting on the object causes it to move from one place to another.

The work $W$ done by the force $F$ on the object as it undergoes a displacement $d$ is: 
$$
W=Fd\cos{\theta}
$$
where $\theta$ (0° to 180°) is the angle **between** the direction of the force and displacement.
![[Drawing 2025-03-10 22.34.18.excalidraw#^clippedframe=WYGzqqqECnDb6TTnZjbSM|700]]
The unit of work is Joule (J) where 1 Joule = 1 newton-meter.
## Work-Energy Relationship
When work is done on the object, the object gains energy. When 100 J of work is done on the object, the object gains 100 J of energy.

The object can store energy in a variety of ways, namely:
- Kinetic Energy
- Gravitational Energy
- Elastic Potential Energy
- Internal Energy

# Kinetic Energy (KE)
The energy an object has because it is moving. 

An object of mass $m$ moving at speed $v$ has a kinetic energy:
$$
KE=\frac{1}{2}mv^2
$$
![[Drawing 2025-03-10 22.34.18.excalidraw#^frame=qL1wVzkhmitY5iGEwd6Ay|700]]
# Gravitational Potential Energy ($PE_{g}$)
The energy an object has because of its separation from the ground. 

An object of mass $m$ separated from the ground a distance $y$ has a gravitational potential energy:
$$
PE_{g}=mgy
$$
The higher from the ground, the bigger the gravitational potential energy.
# Elastic Potential Energy ($PE_{s}$)
The energy stored in a compressed or stretched elastic material such as a spring. 

A spring with force constant $k$ compressed or stretched a distance $x$ from equilibrium position has an elastic potential energy:
$$
PE_{s}=\frac{1}{2}kx^2
$$
# Internal Energy ($PE_s$)
Heat energy due to friction:
$$
E_{int}=F_{k}d
$$
